What Is Neuropathy?

Neuropathy (also called peripheral neuropathy) is a condition that develops when the peripheral nerves become damaged. These nerves form a vast communication network between the brain, spinal cord, and the rest of the body. When they are compromised, the signals they carry become distorted or interrupted entirely.

The result is a range of symptoms that typically begin in the hands and feet: tingling, burning sensations, sharp pain, numbness, and muscle weakness. Over time, untreated neuropathy can lead to balance problems, difficulty walking, and a significant decline in quality of life.

This page focuses on understanding the condition itself. For details on how we treat neuropathy at our St. Peters clinic, visit our neuropathy treatment page.

Types of Neuropathy

Sensory Neuropathy

Affects the nerves that carry sensations such as pain, temperature, and touch. Symptoms include tingling, numbness, and burning pain in the extremities.

Motor Neuropathy

Damages the nerves that control muscle movement, leading to weakness, muscle wasting, and difficulty with coordination and balance.

Autonomic Neuropathy

Affects involuntary body functions such as heart rate, blood pressure, digestion, and bladder control. This type can cause dizziness, digestive issues, and abnormal sweating.

Combination Neuropathy

Many patients experience a mix of sensory, motor, and autonomic nerve damage simultaneously. A comprehensive evaluation is essential for building an effective treatment plan.

Common Causes of Neuropathy

Diabetes

Diabetic neuropathy is the leading cause, responsible for roughly 60 percent of all peripheral neuropathy cases. High blood sugar levels gradually damage the small blood vessels that supply nerves, especially in the feet and lower legs.

Infections

Lyme disease, shingles, hepatitis, and HIV can each trigger nerve inflammation and damage. Post-infectious neuropathy sometimes persists long after the initial illness resolves.

Autoimmune Disorders

Conditions such as lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, and Guillain-Barré syndrome cause the immune system to attack healthy nerve tissue, resulting in progressive damage.

Toxins and Medications

Heavy metals, industrial chemicals, and certain chemotherapy drugs are known to damage peripheral nerves. Chronic alcohol use is also directly toxic to nerve tissue.

Vitamin Deficiencies and Trauma

B-vitamin deficiencies (particularly B12) starve nerves of essential nutrients. Physical trauma from accidents, falls, or repetitive stress injuries can damage nerves at the site of impact.

Symptoms of Neuropathy

Neuropathy symptoms vary depending on which nerves are affected, but commonly include:

  • • Tingling or a pins-and-needles sensation in hands and feet
  • • Numbness or reduced ability to feel pain or temperature changes
  • • Sharp, burning, or stabbing pain (often worse at night)
  • • Muscle weakness and loss of coordination
  • • Extreme sensitivity to touch, even light pressure
  • • Balance problems and difficulty walking
  • • Cramping or muscle twitching

When to Seek Help

Early treatment is critical for neuropathy. The longer nerve damage goes unaddressed, the harder it becomes to manage symptoms and prevent further deterioration. You should seek professional care if you experience:

  • • Persistent tingling or numbness that does not resolve on its own
  • • Pain that interferes with sleep, work, or daily activities
  • • Weakness in your hands or feet that affects grip or balance
  • • Symptoms that are gradually worsening over time
  • • New symptoms appearing in additional areas of the body

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the main cause of neuropathy?
Diabetes is the most common cause of peripheral neuropathy, responsible for roughly 60 percent of all cases. Persistently high blood sugar damages the small blood vessels that supply nerves, particularly in the hands and feet. Other common causes include infections, traumatic injuries, autoimmune disorders, vitamin deficiencies (especially B12), and prolonged exposure to toxins or certain medications.
Can neuropathy be cured?
In most cases, neuropathy is managed rather than cured. Treatment focuses on addressing the underlying cause, relieving symptoms, and preventing further nerve damage. When the root cause is correctable (such as a vitamin deficiency or medication side effect), nerve function may partially or fully recover. Many patients at Morning Star experience meaningful improvement in pain levels, sensation, and mobility with a consistent treatment plan.
What makes neuropathy worse?
Several factors can accelerate nerve damage and worsen symptoms. Poor blood sugar control is the most significant aggravator for diabetic neuropathy. Smoking restricts blood flow to the nerves, alcohol is directly toxic to nerve tissue, and prolonged pressure on specific areas (such as sitting or standing in one position) compounds existing damage. Maintaining a healthy lifestyle and managing underlying conditions is essential to slowing progression.
Can sitting too long cause nerve damage?
Yes, prolonged sitting can contribute to nerve damage through compression. Sitting for extended periods puts pressure on nerves in the legs and lower back, which can lead to numbness, tingling, and pain. This is especially problematic for people who already have neuropathy. Taking regular breaks to stand, stretch, and walk is important for maintaining nerve health and circulation.
