Understanding Sciatica

The sciatic nerve is the longest and thickest nerve in the human body. It runs from the lower back through the hips and buttocks, then branches down each leg. When this nerve is compressed or irritated, the result is a condition commonly called sciatica: pain that radiates from the lower back into one (or occasionally both) legs.

Sciatica is not a diagnosis in itself. It is a symptom of an underlying spinal or muscular condition that puts pressure on the sciatic nerve. The pain can range from a mild, intermittent ache to severe, shooting sensations that make standing or walking extremely difficult. Some patients also experience numbness, tingling, or muscle weakness in the affected leg.

At Morning Star Neuropathy & Pain Treatment Center in St. Peters, MO, we identify the specific cause of your sciatic nerve compression and build a treatment plan that addresses it directly. Rather than relying on pain medication alone, our approach focuses on relieving the pressure on the nerve and promoting long-term recovery.

Common Causes of Sciatica

Herniated Disc

A herniated disc (also called a slipped or ruptured disc) occurs when the soft inner material of a spinal disc pushes through the outer layer and presses on a nearby nerve root. Lumbar disc herniations are the most common cause of sciatica, accounting for roughly 90% of cases. The pain typically worsens with sitting, bending forward, or coughing.

Spinal Stenosis

Lumbar spinal stenosis is a narrowing of the spinal canal in the lower back that compresses the nerve roots, including the sciatic nerve. This condition develops gradually, most often in adults over 60, and tends to cause symptoms that worsen with walking or standing and improve with sitting or leaning forward.

Piriformis Syndrome

The piriformis is a small muscle deep in the buttock that sits directly over the sciatic nerve. When this muscle becomes tight, inflamed, or spasms, it can compress the nerve and produce sciatica-like symptoms. Piriformis syndrome is more common in runners, cyclists, and people who sit for long periods.

Degenerative Disc Disease

As the spinal discs lose moisture and height with age, the space available for nerve roots shrinks. This natural degeneration can lead to bone spurs and disc bulges that put pressure on the sciatic nerve. Degenerative disc disease progresses slowly, so symptoms often build over months or years before patients seek treatment.

Symptoms of Sciatica

Sciatica symptoms typically affect one side of the body. The specific pattern depends on which nerve root is compressed. Common symptoms include:

  • • Sharp, shooting, or burning pain radiating from the lower back down the leg
  • • Numbness or tingling in the leg, foot, or toes
  • • Weakness in the affected leg, making it difficult to lift the foot
  • • Pain that worsens with sitting, coughing, or sneezing
  • • A constant ache on one side of the buttock
  • • Difficulty standing up from a seated position
  • • Pain that improves with walking but flares when standing still
  • • A pins-and-needles sensation along the back of the thigh

If your leg pain is severe, includes progressive weakness, or is accompanied by loss of bladder or bowel control, seek medical attention immediately. These may indicate a serious condition called cauda equina syndrome that requires urgent care.

Sciatica Treatment at Morning Star

Effective sciatica treatment starts with an accurate diagnosis. Our clinical team evaluates your symptoms, reviews imaging when available, and performs targeted assessments to determine exactly where and why the sciatic nerve is being compressed. Treatment is then tailored to the underlying cause.

Neuromodulation Technique

Neuromodulation technique delivers controlled electrical stimulation to disrupt the pain signals traveling along the sciatic nerve. This therapy can significantly reduce radiating leg pain and improve your ability to sit, stand, and walk comfortably, all without surgery or ongoing medication.

Chronic Pain Management

For patients with persistent or recurring sciatica, our chronic pain management program coordinates multiple therapies into a single plan. This may include neuromodulation, targeted stretching protocols, ergonomic guidance, and activity modification designed to relieve nerve pressure and prevent flare-ups.

Regenerative Therapy

When disc degeneration or tissue damage is contributing to your sciatica, regenerative therapy can support the body's natural repair processes at the cellular level. This treatment option is especially valuable for patients who want to avoid surgery while still addressing the structural cause of their nerve compression.

Lifestyle Tips & When to Seek Help

Certain daily habits can make a meaningful difference in managing sciatica symptoms. Regular, gentle movement (walking, swimming) keeps the muscles around the spine flexible and reduces stiffness. Avoiding prolonged sitting, maintaining good posture, and using proper lifting technique all help take pressure off the lower back.

Stretching the hamstrings, hip flexors, and piriformis muscle for a few minutes each day can reduce nerve irritation over time. Sleeping on your side with a pillow between your knees may also relieve nighttime discomfort.

You should seek professional care if your sciatica:

  • • Lasts longer than four to six weeks
  • • Includes progressive weakness in the leg or foot
  • • Makes it difficult to walk, stand, or perform daily activities
  • • Develops suddenly after an injury or fall
  • • Is accompanied by bladder or bowel changes (seek care immediately)

Frequently Asked Questions

What causes sciatica?
Sciatica is most commonly caused by a herniated disc in the lumbar spine pressing on the sciatic nerve. Other causes include spinal stenosis (narrowing of the spinal canal), piriformis syndrome (tightness in the piriformis muscle compressing the nerve), degenerative disc disease, and spondylolisthesis. In rare cases, tumors or infections can also produce sciatic nerve compression.
What can I do for short-term sciatica relief?
Applying ice to the affected area for the first 48 to 72 hours, followed by heat therapy, can help reduce inflammation and relax tight muscles. Gentle stretching (particularly hamstring and piriformis stretches), short walks, and over-the-counter anti-inflammatory medications may provide temporary relief. Prolonged bed rest is not recommended, as it can worsen symptoms. If your pain is severe or does not respond to these measures within a week, a professional evaluation is the next step.
What is the best long-term treatment for sciatica?
The most effective long-term treatment addresses the underlying cause of the nerve compression. At Morning Star, we use a combination of neuromodulation technique, chronic pain management, and regenerative therapy based on your specific diagnosis. These non-invasive approaches target the source of the problem rather than simply managing symptoms, which leads to more lasting relief.
What activities should I avoid with sciatica?
Heavy lifting, prolonged sitting without breaks, high-impact exercises (running on hard surfaces, jumping), and any movements that involve twisting the lower back under load should be avoided or modified during a sciatica flare-up. Forward-bending activities that round the lower back can increase disc pressure and worsen symptoms. Your treatment team can recommend safe alternatives that keep you active without aggravating the nerve.
Can sciatica be cured permanently?
Many patients achieve lasting relief from sciatica with the right treatment plan. When the underlying cause (such as a herniated disc or piriformis tightness) is effectively addressed, the sciatic nerve can heal and symptoms can resolve. However, patients with degenerative spinal conditions may need ongoing management to prevent recurrence. Our goal at Morning Star is to reduce your pain, restore function, and equip you with the tools to maintain your results.
