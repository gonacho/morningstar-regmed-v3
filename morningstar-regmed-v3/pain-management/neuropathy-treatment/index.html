Understanding Neuropathy

Peripheral neuropathy occurs when the nerves outside the brain and spinal cord are damaged, causing weakness, numbness, and pain. Symptoms most often appear in the hands and feet, ranging from mild tingling to debilitating burning sensations and muscle weakness that interfere with daily activities.

The causes are varied. Diabetes accounts for roughly 60% of neuropathy cases, but autoimmune disorders, infections, nutritional deficiencies, and certain medications can also damage nerves over time. Regardless of the cause, the result is the same: your nerves struggle to send accurate signals, and the pain, numbness, or balance problems that follow can limit everything from walking to sleeping.

Who Should Consider Neuropathy Treatment?

If you experience persistent numbness, tingling, or burning in your feet or hands, neuropathy may be the cause. Many patients wait too long before seeking treatment, and nerve damage can progress if left unaddressed.

You should consider scheduling an evaluation if you notice any of the following:

  • • Tingling or pins-and-needles sensations in your extremities
  • • Numbness that makes it difficult to feel your feet while walking
  • • Sharp, shooting, or burning pain in your feet or legs
  • • Difficulty with balance or coordination
  • • Muscle weakness in the affected areas
  • • A diabetes diagnosis with any nerve-related symptoms

Early treatment gives your nerves the best chance of recovery. The team at Morning Star is here to help patients throughout St. Peters and the surrounding communities take that first step.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the main cause of neuropathy?
Diabetes is the most common cause of peripheral neuropathy, accounting for roughly 60% of cases. Chronically elevated blood sugar damages the small blood vessels that supply nerves, leading to tingling, numbness, and pain, especially in the feet and hands. Other causes include autoimmune disorders, infections, nutritional deficiencies, and certain medications.
Can neuropathy be cured?
In most cases, neuropathy is managed rather than cured. However, early intervention can slow progression, significantly reduce symptoms, and in some instances partially reverse nerve damage. Our treatment plans focus on addressing the underlying cause, restoring nerve function, and improving your quality of life through therapies such as neuromodulation and regenerative options.
What makes neuropathy worse?
Poor blood sugar control is the leading factor that worsens neuropathy, particularly in diabetic patients. Smoking restricts blood flow to the nerves, alcohol is directly toxic to nerve tissue, and nutritional deficiencies (especially B vitamins) accelerate damage. Avoiding these triggers is a critical part of any effective treatment plan.
Can prolonged sitting cause nerve damage?
Yes. Sitting for extended periods can compress the sciatic nerve, peroneal nerve, and other peripheral nerves, leading to numbness, tingling, or pain in the legs and feet. Over time, chronic compression may contribute to lasting nerve injury. Regular movement breaks and ergonomic adjustments can help reduce this risk.
